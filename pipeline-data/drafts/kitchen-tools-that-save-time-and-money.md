You can cook well without a kitchen full of single-use gadgets. What actually helps is a short list of tools that get used over and over: they save time, reduce frustration, and sometimes save money because you're more likely to cook instead of ordering in. Here's a practical list, not a shopping spree.

## A Good Chef's Knife

One sharp, comfortable chef's knife does most of the work: chopping onions, cutting vegetables, trimming meat. You don't need a full block. A 8-inch blade that fits your hand is enough. Keep it sharp with a basic honing rod or a simple sharpener. A dull knife is slower and more dangerous than a sharp one. This one tool might save more time than anything else in the kitchen.

## A Large Cutting Board

A big cutting board gives you room to prep without crowding. Wood or a good plastic board that doesn't slide around is fine. If you have space, keep it out so it's easy to grab. That small step can make starting dinner feel less like a project.

## A Can Opener That Works

It sounds obvious, but a can opener that actually works and doesn't leave jagged edges is worth having. You'll use it for beans, tomatoes, and broth. A basic manual one is cheap; if you use cans a lot, a decent one pays for itself in less frustration.

## Measuring Cups and Spoons

Eyeballing is fine for a lot of things, but when a recipe needs a specific amount of baking powder or you're tracking portions, you need something reliable. A set of measuring cups and spoons doesn't cost much and avoids wasted batches and guesswork.

## A Sheet Pan or Two

Sheet pans are for more than cookies. You can roast vegetables, bake chicken, and do one-pan dinners. They're easy to clean and stack. Having two means you can roast two trays at once or keep one free while the other is in the oven.

## A Strainer or Colander

Draining pasta, rinsing beans, washing greens: a strainer or colander is used constantly. Get one that sits steadily in the sink and doesn't tip over when it's full.

You don't have to buy everything at once. Start with a knife and cutting board, then add as you notice what's slowing you down. The goal is fewer tools that actually earn their place, not a drawer full of stuff you never use.
