Canned beans are convenient, but dried beans cost less and let you control the texture and salt. Cooking them from scratch isn't complicated: you soak, you simmer, you season. Once you've done it a few times, it's as routine as boiling pasta. Here's a method that works for black beans, pinto beans, chickpeas, and most others.

## Rinse and Soak

Pick through the beans and remove any stones or broken pieces. Rinse them in a colander under cold water. Soaking shortens cooking time and can make them easier to digest. Put the beans in a large pot and cover with plenty of cold water (at least a few inches above the beans). Soak for 8 to 12 hours, or overnight. If you're in a hurry, you can do a quick soak: bring them to a boil for 2 minutes, turn off the heat, cover, and let them sit for an hour. Drain and rinse before cooking.

## Cook Low and Steady

Put the soaked, drained beans in a pot and cover with fresh water (again, a few inches above the beans). Bring to a boil, then reduce the heat to a gentle simmer. Skim off any foam at the top. Partially cover the pot so steam can escape. Cook until the beans are tender. Black beans and pinto beans often take 1 to 1.5 hours; chickpeas can take 1.5 to 2 hours. Start checking at 45 minutes and taste a bean. Add more water if the level gets low. Don't add salt or acidic ingredients (like tomatoes or vinegar) until the beans are almost tender, or they can stay firm longer.

## When to Add Salt and Flavor

Once the beans are soft, add salt to taste. You can also add garlic, onion, bay leaves, or a strip of kombu during cooking if you like. If you're using the beans in a recipe that has its own seasoning, you can keep the pot plain and season later. The beans will keep in their cooking liquid in the fridge for several days, or you can drain and freeze them in portions.

## No Soak (If You Prefer)

Some people skip soaking and just cook the beans longer. It works, but it can take 2 hours or more depending on the type. If you have time and don't mind the longer cook, you can add dried beans straight to the pot with plenty of water and simmer until tender. Soaking is a shortcut, not a requirement.

You'll get more beans from a bag of dried than from the same cost in cans, and you can cook a big batch once and use it all week in tacos, soups, and salads. It's one of those skills that pays off without a lot of fuss.
