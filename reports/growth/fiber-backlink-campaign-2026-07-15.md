# Fiber Backlink Campaign, 2026-07-15

## Executive decision

The strongest linkable asset isn't another general article about eating on a budget. It's the open, checkable dataset behind the site's fiber coverage: 53 common grocery foods ranked by grams of fiber per dollar, using USDA FoodData Central values and July 2026 shelf prices.

The campaign should lead with the data, not with a request for a backlink. The useful hook is simple:

- Dry green split peas deliver about 71.0 grams of fiber per dollar.
- Fresh blueberries deliver about 2.5 grams per dollar.
- That's a 28-times price gap for the same tracked nutrient.
- The raw CSV, ranking, calculation method, and practical meal examples are public.

Primary asset:

- https://www.daily-life-hacks.com/fiber-per-dollar-cheapest-high-fiber-foods/

Supporting assets:

- https://www.daily-life-hacks.com/how-to-eat-more-fiber-on-a-budget-complete-guide/
- https://www.daily-life-hacks.com/one-dollar-fiber-what-it-buys/
- https://www.daily-life-hacks.com/data/fiber-per-dollar-2026.csv

The live Bing Webmaster review on 2026-07-15 showed 93 Copilot citations in seven days for the budget-fiber pillar. That is a useful internal prioritization signal. It should not appear in an outreach claim until a dated export or screenshot is stored with the campaign evidence.

No outreach was sent while preparing this file.

## What counts as success

A sent email isn't a result. A reply isn't yet a link. The campaign uses the following ladder:

| Stage | Evidence required | 30-day target |
|---|---|---:|
| Researched | Current relevance page and public contact route verified | 25 prospects |
| Ready | Personalized draft passes the human review gate | 12 pitches |
| Sent | Timestamp and exact recipient recorded | 12 sends |
| Engaged | A human reply or editorial follow-up | 3 replies |
| Used | Prospect cites, embeds, or discusses the dataset | 2 uses |
| Earned link | Live followed or nofollowed editorial link from a new referring domain | 2 links |
| Search effect | GSC/Bing query and landing-page comparison after 28 days | Directional, not guaranteed |

The first 12 targets are intentionally split between resource curators and journalists. Resource links may be slower but durable. Journalism links can arrive faster, but the story must fit an active editorial need.

## Send sequence

### Wave 1: education and resource curators, days 1 to 3

Send FB-001 through FB-006 individually. These organizations already publish budget-food, whole-grain, pulse, or fiber teaching material. The ask is to review an open dataset as a possible resource, not to endorse Daily Life Hacks.

### Wave 2: grocery and consumer editors, days 4 to 6

Send FB-007 through FB-012 individually. Lead with the surprising data point that matches each editor's beat. Offer the CSV, chart, and methodology. Don't attach files on first contact unless the outlet requests them.

### Follow-up, day 10 to 13

One follow-up per prospect, seven calendar days after the original note. Add one new piece of value, such as a two-row comparison, a chart link, or a relevant local angle. Don't send a second follow-up.

### Expansion, days 14 to 30

Use FB-013 through FB-025 only after reviewing the first-wave response pattern. If extension teams respond and trade editors don't, move toward education partners. If editors ask for a fresh price update, publish a dated refresh before continuing.

## Human review gate before any send

1. Re-open the prospect's relevance and contact pages on the day of sending.
2. Confirm the named person still holds the listed role.
3. Open all four Daily Life Hacks assets and confirm they return 200.
4. Verify the 71.0, 2.5, and 28-times figures against the live table and CSV.
5. Add the sender's real name, role, and relationship to Daily Life Hacks.
6. Remove any sentence that sounds like a request to promote the site.
7. Don't claim medical outcomes, credentialed expertise, peer review, or institutional endorsement.
8. Don't describe the 88-cent sample day as a complete daily food budget. It prices the fiber-carrying ingredients only.
9. Don't hide the use of Walmart listings or the July 2026 date. Price data gets stale.
10. For Civil Eats and AARP, don't send an AI-written pitch. Their published policies require or strongly favor human-authored work. Use the research record only, and rewrite from scratch.

## Risks and stop rules

- Stop using any address that bounces. Don't guess a replacement email.
- Stop after one follow-up if there is no reply.
- Remove any prospect that requires payment, a reciprocal link, or an undisclosed commercial arrangement.
- Don't pitch the same story to outlets that require exclusivity.
- Don't submit a bylined article under David Miller's name unless the human author approves and owns the final text.
- Refresh the price dataset before using it after 2026-09-30.
- If fewer than two of the first 12 prospects reply, don't scale the same message to the remaining 13. Interview the response pattern and rewrite the offer.

The campaign's job is to make a useful dataset easier for the right people to discover and cite. The backlink is the receipt, not the opening line.
