I used to just buy a bunch of cilantro, toss it directly into the crisper drawer in its little plastic produce bag, and then act shocked when it turned into a puddle of green slime three days later. Honestly, I must have thrown away hundreds of dollars in ruined herbs before I finally figured out the right way to handle them. The broader [food-waste guide](/how-to-reduce-food-waste-at-home-easy-tips/) uses the same unglamorous rule: make the food last long enough to actually eat it.

The biggest mistake we make with herbs is storing them all exactly the same. But here's the secret: herbs fall into two different categories, and if you want to know how to store fresh herbs to last longer, you have to handle them according to their type.

## The Soft Herbs (The Bouquet Method)

Soft herbs are the ones with tender green stems. Think cilantro, parsley, basil, and dill. Store these like a bouquet of fresh flowers.

1. **The Trim:** As soon as you get home from the store, leave the rubber band on the bunch. Take a sharp pair of kitchen scissors and snip about half an inch off the absolute bottom of the stems. 
2. **The Jar:** Fill a small glass jar or a heavy water glass with about an inch of cold water. Place the bunch of herbs right into the water so just the freshly cut ends are submerged. Make sure no actual leaves are touching the water, or they will rot instantly.
3. **The Tent:** Take a small plastic sandwich bag (or even the produce bag they came in) and loosely drape it over the top of the herbs like a little greenhouse tent. 
4. **The Location:** Put cilantro and parsley right into the fridge. They can last two to three weeks like this. **Exception:** Basil hates the cold. Keep it in a flower-style jar, but leave the jar sitting out on your kitchen counter at room temperature.

A jar of living cilantro and scallions also means weeknight stir fry gets its proper fresh finish. These [quick and easy stir-fry sauce recipes](/quick-and-easy-stir-fry-sauce-recipes-no-bottled-stuff/) handle the rest of the equation. 

## The Hard Herbs (The Damp Towel Method)

Hard herbs have tough, woody stems. This includes rosemary, thyme, sage, and oregano. These don't need a jar of water; in fact, too much moisture will ruin them. 

1. **The Wrap:** Run a single paper towel under the tap, and squeeze out as much water as you can. It should feel barely damp, not wet.
2. **The Roll:** Lay the woody herbs flat on the damp paper towel and roll them up gently like a little burrito. 
3. **The Seal:** Slide the wrapped herbs into a zip-lock bag or an airtight glass container and pop them in the crisper drawer. The tiny bit of moisture keeps them from drying out, but the plastic protects them from excessive fridge humidity. 

Learning how to store fresh herbs to last longer changes the way you cook. You can finally garnish your tacos on a Friday with the same cilantro you bought on Monday! It also means herb-heavy dishes like this [high fiber bulgur tabbouleh salad](/tabbouleh-salad-high-fiber-bulgur/) stop feeling like a race against the crisper drawer.

## Should you wash herbs before storing them?

Waiting to wash herbs until you use them is the simplest option. Extra water trapped between leaves speeds up sliminess, especially in dense bunches of cilantro and parsley. If the herbs arrive muddy and washing now makes more sense, rinse them in cold water and dry them thoroughly in a salad spinner. Spread them on a clean towel for another ten minutes before storage.

Remove any yellow, bruised, or slimy leaves while you are there. One collapsing sprig holds moisture against its neighbors and makes the whole bunch deteriorate faster. This is not glamorous work, but neither is pulling liquefied parsley from the bottom of a produce bag.

## Check the jar instead of forgetting it

Change the water every two or three days, or sooner if it turns cloudy. Trim the stems again if the ends look dark. Keep the bag loose over the leaves so moisture can escape, and move the jar away from the coldest back wall of the refrigerator where tender leaves may freeze.

Basil is the exception worth remembering. Keep it at room temperature, out of harsh direct sun. Cold air darkens basil leaves quickly. If the bunch starts to droop, trim the stems and replace the water just as you would with flowers.

## Freeze the herbs you won't use fresh

Freezing preserves cooking flavor better than fresh texture. Chop parsley, cilantro, dill, or chives, pack them into an ice cube tray, cover with water or olive oil, and freeze. Transfer the solid cubes to a labeled bag. Drop them into soups, sauces, and skillets, not onto a finished salad where limp leaves have nowhere to hide.

Rosemary and thyme can be frozen on their stems in a sealed bag. Once frozen, the leaves often strip away easily. For another small freezer habit that saves produce, use the tray method in this guide to [freezing bananas for smoothies](/how-to-freeze-bananas-for-smoothies/).
