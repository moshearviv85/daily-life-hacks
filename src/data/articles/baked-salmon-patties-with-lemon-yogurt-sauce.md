Here's the thing about salmon patties: most recipes assume you want to stand over a hot skillet, wrestling with delicate fish that falls apart the moment you try to flip it. And sure, pan-frying can work. But it creates a mess, stresses you out, and often leaves the center underdone while the outside gets too dark. Baking them solves all of that, and once you try it, you won't go back.

These baked salmon patties have a crisp edge, a tender middle, and they hold together well enough to eat with your hands if you're feeling informal. The lemon yogurt sauce isn't optional-it's the whole reason the dish works. It cuts through the richness, adds brightness, and makes something that could feel heavy feel light instead.

## Choosing Your Salmon

You can absolutely use fresh salmon here, but honestly, canned works just as well and costs less. I reach for the pink salmon in the 14.75-ounce cans. It's already cooked, already flaked, and it mixes into the patty binding without any extra work. The bones-if you're using whole salmon rather than filleted-dissolve into nothing when you mix the patties. Most people can't even detect them, and they're actually a decent source of calcium. If the idea weirds you out, look for boneless canned salmon, though you'll pay a small premium.

When you're standing in the canned fish aisle, grab a few cans and check the sodium. Some brands add a lot of salt, which you'll then have to account for when seasoning the patties. Low-sodium is always an option, though it costs slightly more. The difference in the finished dish is noticeable if you're sensitive to salt.

If you're using fresh salmon fillets, figure on about 12 ounces raw. Bake or poach it first until it flakes easily, then let it cool before mixing. The texture will be slightly more luxurious than canned, and you can season it more precisely as it cooks. One tip: don't overcook the fresh salmon in this preliminary step. It should be just done, not dried out, because it's going in the oven again. Pull it at about 125°F internal temperature and let carryover cooking finish the job.

One thing to avoid: salmon that's already heavily seasoned or smoked. It will compete with the other flavors in the patty and the sauce. Stick to plain. This isn't the place for that fancy oak-smoked stuff you might put on a bagel.

## Building the Patty Mixture

The binding is simple: egg, breadcrumbs, and the natural stickiness of the salmon itself. Don't overmix. Use your hands and fold everything together just until it holds. Overworking it makes the patties tough, and nobody wants a dense fish hockey puck.

The red onion adds a sharp bite that balances the fish. Dice it fine-quarter-inch pieces or smaller-so you don't get a huge chunk that overwhelms a bite. You could use green onion instead if you want something milder, but red gives you more contrast. If you're cooking for kids who are onion-averse, swap in a couple tablespoons of finely minced celery. It won't have the same bite, but it adds crunch and some extra vegetable content.

The dill is non-negotiable in my kitchen. It pairs with salmon the way mint pairs with lamb-something clicks. If you only have dried dill, use about a teaspoon, but the flavor won't be as bright. Fresh is worth the small extra effort. Grab a bunch at the store, use what you need, and stick the rest in a glass of water in the fridge. It keeps for about a week this way.

The garlic powder and paprika are there for depth, not dominance. You're not making a heavily spiced dish. The paprika adds a subtle warmth and color, but it shouldn't taste like you're eating Hungarian paprika. A half-teaspoon is plenty. If you're unsure, start with less and add more to taste after you've mixed everything.

Let the mixture sit for 20 minutes before you shape it. This is important. The breadcrumbs soak up some of the moisture, the flavors meld, and the patties will hold together better during baking. If you're in a rush, you can skip this, but I've had patties crumble on the sheet pan because I was impatient. The resting time is worth it. While you're waiting, preheat the oven and get your baking sheet ready.

## Shaping and Spacing

Eight patties from this batch is the right number-about three-quarters of an inch thick each. Any thinner and they'll dry out. Any thicker and the center won't cook through before the edges brown. If you want fewer, larger patties, increase the cooking time by about 4 minutes and check the internal temperature.

Space them out on the baking sheet. Give each one at least an inch of room. They won't spread dramatically, but they need airflow to get crispy on all sides. Crowding them steams them instead of baking them, and you lose that nice golden edge. If your baking sheet isn't big enough, use two. It's worth the extra pan to get the texture right.

Press them down slightly after you place them on the sheet. They tend to dome up a bit when you shape them, and a flatter patty cooks more evenly. A simple press with your palm does the job. Don't compress them too hard though-you still want some give in the center.

One more tip: wet your hands with cold water before shaping. The mixture won't stick to your palms as much, and you'll get cleaner edges on each patty. This sounds like a small thing, but it makes a noticeable difference in how professional they look.

## The Lemon Yogurt Sauce

This sauce is the thing that makes the dish. Without it, you've got a decent salmon cake. With it, you've got something worth making on a weeknight.

Greek yogurt is the base-it's thick enough to hold on the patty, tangy enough to cut the fish, and has enough body to feel substantial. Plain, not flavored. Never flavored. Lemon zest and juice go in raw, which gives you that bright, clean citrus note. If you cook the lemon, it loses its snap. Add the zest first, then the juice, and whisk vigorously to combine.

A little olive oil smooths everything out and adds a touch of richness. The sauce should be pourable but not runny. If it's too thick, add a teaspoon of water. Too thin, stir in another tablespoon of yogurt. You're looking for a consistency that coats the back of a spoon but still drips slowly.

The salt in the sauce matters more than you might think. It pulls the tang forward and balances the creaminess. Don't skip it, even though the recipe says "a pinch." That pinch is doing real work.

You can make this ahead. It keeps in the fridge for three days, though the lemon flavor softens a bit over time. Give it a stir before you serve. If it's been in the fridge and seems too thick, let it sit at room temperature for 10 minutes or add a splash of water to loosen it up.

## Baking Technique and Timing

Four hundred degrees. That's the temperature. Any lower and the patties take too long to set. Any higher and the outside overcooks before the center is done. If your oven runs hot, drop it to 385°F and add a couple minutes. You'll learn your oven's quirks over time.

Sixteen to eighteen minutes, flipping halfway through. The halfway flip is worth doing-it ensures even browning on both sides. Use a wide spatula and slide it under each patty rather than trying to lift from below. Less handling means less breaking. Here's a trick: let them sit for about 8 minutes before you flip. They release more easily from the parchment that way.

They're done when the edges are golden brown and the centers feel firm when you press lightly. If you want to be precise, an instant-read thermometer inserted into the center should read about 145°F. But you can also just trust the visual cues-golden edges and firm centers don't lie.

Let them rest on the pan for two minutes after pulling them out. They tighten up as they cool, which helps them hold together when you plate them. This is a good time to give the sauce one final whisk and check your plating situation.

## Serving Ideas

These patties are flexible. Serve them on a plate with the sauce drizzled over and a simple green salad on the side. Arugula works particularly well because its pepperiness complements the fish. Throw in some cherry tomatoes and a light vinaigrette, and you've got a complete meal.

Put them on a bun like a salmon burger-add some arugula and the yogurt sauce for brightness. Toast the bun first, because a soft bun with a crispy patty is an uneven textural experience. If you're going the burger route, consider adding a slice of red onion for extra bite.

Tuck them into a tortilla with shredded cabbage and a squeeze of lime. The cabbage adds crunch and soaks up any stray sauce. This makes for a good lunch the next day if you've stored everything properly.

A few lemon wedges on the table let people add more brightness if they want it. Some people want that extra hit. Don't judge them. A small dish of capers alongside is also nice for the people who want more briny flavor.

If you're serving these at a dinner party, plate them individually with a quenelle of sauce rather than drizzling. It looks more intentional. Garnish with a small sprig of fresh dill. The plate looks better, and it signals that you put thought into the presentation.

## Storage and Reheating

In the fridge, they'll last three days in an airtight container. The sauce goes in a separate container so the patties don't get soggy. This matters more than people realize. If you store them together, the sauce will loosen the breadcrumb coating and by day two, you'll be dealing with a mushy situation.

Reheating is where most people go wrong. Microwaving makes them rubbery. The best approach is a sheet pan in a 375°F oven for about eight minutes. That restores some crispness to the edges without drying out the interior. If they're straight from the fridge, add 2-3 minutes. The key is checking at the 6-minute mark so you don't overdo it.

Freezing works too. Bake the patties, cool them completely, then freeze flat on a sheet pan before transferring to a container. They'll keep for two months. Thaw overnight in the fridge before reheating the same way-sheet pan, 375°F, about ten minutes since they're starting cold. The sauce doesn't freeze well, so make fresh sauce when you're ready to serve.

One thing to know: frozen and reheated patties will never be quite as good as fresh. The texture changes slightly. They're still good, but they're not the same. If you're planning to freeze, make the recipe with fresh salmon rather than canned for the best result after reheating.

## Variations Worth Trying

Once you've got the base recipe down, there are a few directions you can take it. Add some capers to the patty mixture for a briny, more sophisticated flavor-about a tablespoon, drained. Fold in some finely chopped celery for extra crunch. Swap the dill for tarragon if you want something more French.

For the sauce, mix in some fresh herbs-chives, parsley, even a little mint. A pinch of cayenne gives it some heat without making it spicy. Some people like a bit of honey in the sauce for sweetness; I don't do it, but I'm not going to stop you. If you try the honey version, start with half a teaspoon and taste as you go.

You could make this with tuna instead of salmon. Same method, slightly different flavor profile-more mild, less fishy. It's a solid backup if salmon isn't on sale. Canned tuna is usually cheaper than salmon, so this can be a budget-friendly pivot.

If you want to add some extra vegetables to the patties, finely grated zucchini works well. Squeeze out the excess water first or your mixture will be too wet. About a quarter cup of grated zucchini added to the mix gives you extra nutrition and a softer texture.

The real reason these work is that they're easy enough for a Tuesday but impressive enough for company. You can get them in the oven in fifteen minutes, they're mostly hands-off while they bake, and the sauce comes together in the time it takes the patties to finish. There's no splattering oil, no finicky flipping, no stress. You just mix, shape, bake, and sauce. That's dinner.
