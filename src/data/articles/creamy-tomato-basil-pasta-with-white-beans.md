  - 12 oz short pasta (penne, rigatoni, or fusilli)
  - 1 can (15 oz) cannellini or great northern beans, drained and rinsed
  - 1 can (28 oz) crushed tomatoes
  - 3 cloves garlic, minced
  - 1/2 cup white wine (or chicken broth)
  - 1/2 cup reserved pasta water
  - 1/2 cup freshly grated Parmesan cheese, plus more for serving
  - 2 tbsp olive oil
  - 1 tsp sugar (optional, balances acidity)
  - 1/2 tsp red pepper flakes
  - Salt and freshly ground black pepper to taste

steps:
  - Bring a large pot of salted water to boil. Cook pasta according to package directions until al dente. Reserve 1/2 cup pasta water before draining.
  - While pasta cooks, heat olive oil in a large skillet over medium heat. Add garlic and red pepper flakes, sauté until fragrant, about 1 minute.
  - Pour in white wine or broth and let it reduce by half, about 2 minutes.
  - Add crushed tomatoes, sugar if using, salt, and pepper. Simmer for 8-10 minutes until slightly thickened.
  - Stir in beans and cook for 3-4 minutes until warmed through.
  - Add drained pasta to the sauce. Toss everything together, adding reserved pasta water as needed to reach desired consistency.
  - Remove from heat. Stir in Parmesan cheese and fresh basil. Serve immediately with extra Parmesan on the side.

Here's the thing about pasta with beans: it sounds like something you'd find on a health-food blog, all "meatless Monday" and virtue. But this dish doesn't need to audition for anything. It's just a really good weeknight dinner that happens to come together in one pot, uses stuff you probably already have, and doesn't require you to think about whether you're being "good."

That's the version I want to give you.

## Why White Beans Replace Cream Without Anyone Noticing

The trick here is that white beans-cannellini, great northern, even navy-have a mild, slightly earthy flavor that disappears into a sauce while adding body. Blend them with a little pasta water and you get something that looks and feels like cream without any dairy. It's not an exact swap in texture, but it's close enough that nobody at the table will ask what you did.

What you get is a sauce that clings to the pasta, has some weight to it, and lets the tomato flavor come through clean. The beans add a subtle nuttiness that pairs with basil in a way that feels intentional, not like you're eating a compromise.

This works because the beans aren't trying to be cream. They're being themselves, and the sauce benefits.

## Picking Your Pasta Shape

Here's where you can actually screw this up. Some shapes hold sauce better than others, and since you're not making a heavy meat sauce with chunks, you need geometry on your side.

Short, tube, or ridged shapes are your friends here. Penne, rigatoni, fusilli, orecchiette-these catch the sauce in their nooks and deliver more flavor per bite. Long strand pasta like spaghetti works, but you get less sauce-to-pasta contact and more sliding-around-the-bowl energy, which is less satisfying.

If you only have spaghetti on hand, it will be fine. But if you're going to the store specifically for this, grab a short shape. Your sauce will thank you.

## The Canned Tomato Question

You need crushed tomatoes for this- San Marzano if you can find them, but don't make a special trip. Generic grocery-store crushed tomatoes work perfectly well. The difference is subtle: San Marzano are sweeter and less acidic, which matters if you're sensitive to that bright tomato tang. Regular crushed tomatoes will give you a more aggressive tomato flavor that mellows out as it simmers.

Don't use whole peeled tomatoes here because you'll need to break them up anyway, and don't use diced tomatoes unless you want a chunky texture. Crushed is the move.

One thing to know: canned tomatoes almost always need salt. They come packed in liquid that's under-seasoned, and your sauce will taste flat if you don't add some. Start with half a teaspoon of salt when you add the tomatoes, then adjust at the end.

## When to Add the Beans

This is the step most people get wrong. You add the beans too early and they break down into mush, turning your sauce gray and gritty. Add them too late and they're just cold white lumps in warm pasta.

The sweet spot: add them after the tomato sauce has simmered for 8-10 minutes and thickened slightly. Then let them cook in the sauce for 3-4 minutes. They're heated through but still hold their shape. When you toss everything together, some will break up and add body, while others stay intact for texture.

If you're using canned beans, make sure to drain and rinse them first. The liquid they pack in is starchy and can make your sauce gummy instead of smooth.

## The Garlic and Oil Moment

Here's where the recipe starts to smell like dinner. You heat olive oil in a large skillet-nonstick works fine here-and add your minced garlic and red pepper flakes. The oil needs to be hot enough that the garlic sizzles immediately, but not so hot that it browns before you add the next ingredient.

About 30 seconds after the garlic hits the oil, you pour in the white wine. If you don't keep wine in the house, chicken broth works, but the wine adds a subtle acidity that brightens the whole dish. You let it reduce by half, which takes maybe two minutes, and then you're ready for the tomatoes.

This step is non-negotiable for flavor. Skipping the wine and just adding tomatoes to the garlic skips the layer that makes the sauce taste like it came from a restaurant instead of a box.

## Choosing Your Wine and Oil

A quick note on the wine: you don't need to spend money here. A mid-range bottle you'd actually drink works, but so does the cheap cooking wine sold next to the olive oil. The alcohol cooks off entirely, so you're left with acidity and subtle flavor. If you want to get slightly fancy, a Pinot Grigio or unoaked Chardonnay adds fruitiness. Avoid oaked whites-they can make the sauce taste muddy.

For the olive oil, use what you'd dress a salad with. If your olive oil tastes like nothing or burns easily, the dish will suffer. Extra virgin is fine here, but don't use the expensive single-estate stuff you've been saving. A solid everyday olive oil is all this recipe needs.

## The Pasta Water Secret

You need to save half a cup of pasta water before you drain the pasta. This is the most useful hack in this entire recipe, and most people throw it down the drain without thinking.

Pasta water is starchy and slightly salty. When you add it to the sauce, it loosens things up and helps the sauce cling to the pasta. You don't want a soup, but you want the sauce to be loose enough to coat everything evenly. The starch in the water also helps the sauce emulsify, making it look silky instead of separated.

Start with a quarter cup, toss the pasta in, and see how it looks. Add more if needed. You'll learn to read the consistency after making this a few times.

## Why Fresh Basil Matters

Dried basil is useless here. It tastes like hay, and it won't give you that bright, slightly peppery hit that makes this dish feel like summer. Fresh basil, torn rather than cut, gets added at the end-after you take the pan off the heat.

The residual warmth is enough to wilt the basil slightly and release its oils without cooking the life out of it. If you add it while the sauce is still boiling, it turns black and bitter within seconds. Take the pan off the heat first.

A half cup might sound like a lot for four servings, but it shrinks significantly once it hits the hot pasta. Don't be shy with it.

## The Cheese Question

Parmesan is the move here, and you want the real stuff if possible. The aged, waxy kind you find in the grocery store's cheese section, not the green tub of powdered anything. A half cup stirred in at the end adds salt, umami, and a slight melt that makes the sauce feel complete.

If you're feeding vegetarians, check the Parmesan label. Some brands use animal rennet, which means it's not vegetarian even though it looks like cheese. Look for "vegetarian Parmesan" or "enzyme-free" on the label, or just grab the pre-shredded stuff that usually avoids it.

You could swap in Pecorino Romano for a sharper, saltier kick. It works well with the tomato and basil, though it's more aggressive. Half Parmesan, half Pecorino is a solid compromise if you want to experiment.

## How to Keep This Vegan or Dairy-Free

The recipe as written uses Parmesan, which adds that salty, umami depth that makes the sauce feel complete. If you need to skip dairy, you have a few options.

Skip the Parmesan entirely and add a squeeze of lemon juice at the end. The acid will wake up the flavors the same way the cheese would. You could also add a tablespoon of nutritional yeast, which gives a cheesy vibe without the dairy, though it's an acquired taste.

The beans still provide creaminess regardless. You won't get the exact same result, but you'll get something that's still satisfying and doesn't taste like you're eating a apology.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Overcooking the pasta is the biggest one. Remember that the pasta cooks for another minute or two when you toss it with the hot sauce. Pull it from the boiling water when it's slightly underdone-it should have a tiny white dot in the center when you bite into it.

Another mistake: adding the cheese too early. Parmesan should go in after you take the pan off the heat. If you add it while the sauce is still bubbling, the cheese clumps and melts into stringy globs instead of distributing evenly. Low and slow with the cheese.

Finally, don't skip the sugar. You only need a teaspoon, and it's not there to make the dish sweet. It neutralizes the acidity from the canned tomatoes, which can taste harsh if you don't balance it. This is a trick Italian cooks have used for decades. Try the sauce with and without-you'll notice the difference.

## Storing and Reheating

This pasta keeps well for 3-4 days in the fridge, stored in an airtight container. The sauce thickens significantly overnight as the pasta absorbs liquid. When you reheat it, don't microwave it dry-add a splash of water or broth to the container first.

The best way to reheat is in a pan over medium heat with a few tablespoons of water. Stir occasionally for 3-4 minutes until everything is heated through and the sauce has loosened up again. The pasta will continue absorbing sauce as it sits, so don't worry if it seems a little wet when you first add the water. It dries out as it cooks.

Freezing is possible but not ideal. The texture of the beans changes when frozen and thawed, getting slightly grainy. It's fine in a pinch, but fresh is always better.

## Scaling This Recipe

The recipe serves four as written, but you can easily scale it up or down. For a quick lunch for two, halve everything and use a smaller pan. For a dinner party, double the batch but use your largest skillet-you'll need the surface area to toss the pasta without it flying out onto the stove.

One thing to watch when scaling: the sauce-to-pasta ratio stays the same, but you may need more pasta water to get the consistency right. Larger batches have more surface area, which means more evaporation. Start with less water than you think you need and add gradually.

## Serving This Without Apology

This pasta doesn't need a salad to be a complete meal, but a simple green salad with lemon vinaigrette cuts through the richness nicely. Some crusty bread for dipping in the sauce is almost required. If you want to add protein, leftover rotisserie chicken or cooked Italian sausage are the easiest additions-just warm them in the pan with the beans.

The thing is, you don't need to justify this dinner to anyone. It's not trying to be healthy or virtuous or impressive. It's a solid, satisfying meal that uses canned beans and tomatoes and whatever pasta you have, comes together in half an hour, and tastes like you put more effort into it than you did. That's the kind of weeknight cooking that actually makes your life easier.
