- 2 ripe but firm pears (e.g., Anjou, Bosc), cored and thinly sliced
- 4 cups mixed greens (spring mix, arugula, or baby spinach)
- 1/2 cup crumbled blue cheese (Gorgonzola or Roquefort)
- 1/2 cup walnuts, roughly chopped
- 2 tablespoons olive oil
- 1 tablespoon balsamic vinegar
- 1 teaspoon Dijon mustard
- 1 teaspoon honey or maple syrup
- Salt and freshly ground black pepper to taste
- 'Optional: 1/4 cup dried cranberries or pomegranate seeds for extra tartness'
steps:
- Preheat oven to 350°F (175°C). Spread walnuts on a baking sheet and toast for 5-7
  minutes, until fragrant. Watch carefully to prevent burning. Let cool.
- In a small bowl, whisk together the olive oil, balsamic vinegar, Dijon mustard,
  and honey (or maple syrup). Season with salt and pepper. This is your vinaigrette,
  obviously.
- In a large salad bowl, combine the mixed greens. Add the sliced pears, toasted walnuts,
  and crumbled blue cheese.
- Drizzle the vinaigrette over the salad components. Gently toss everything together
  until well combined and coated.
- Serve immediately. If using, sprinkle with optional dried cranberries or pomegranate
  seeds right before serving for a little extra pop.

This pear and walnut salad works because every ingredient argues with the next one in a useful way. The pear is sweet, the blue cheese is salty and sharp, the walnuts crunch, and the balsamic dressing keeps the whole bowl from tasting like fruit wandered into lunch by mistake.

It takes about ten minutes of hands-on work. Toast the walnuts, whisk the dressing, slice the pears, and toss. The only real danger is walking away from the oven and returning to a tray of tiny walnut briquettes.

## Which pears are firm enough for salad?

Use Bosc or Anjou pears that give slightly when you press near the stem. A rock-hard pear won't bring much sweetness. A very soft one will collapse as soon as you toss the greens, which is less salad and more an administrative problem.

Leave the peel on. It adds color and texture, and it keeps you from turning a simple recipe into a fruit-peeling project. Slice each pear thinly so you get some fruit, cheese, and walnut in the same bite instead of wrestling with a pear wedge the size of a doorstop.

## Why should you toast the walnuts first?

Five to seven minutes at 350°F makes the walnuts smell nuttier and taste less flat. Spread them in one layer and check at the five-minute mark. Nuts can go from pale to burned while you're answering one text message.

Let them cool before they hit the greens. Hot walnuts can wilt the leaves and soften the blue cheese. You want distinct pieces here, not a warm green heap.

## How do you balance the pear salad dressing?

The dressing uses 2 tablespoons olive oil, 1 tablespoon balsamic vinegar, Dijon, and a small spoonful of honey or maple syrup. That's enough sweetness. The pears are already doing that job, and the dressing doesn't need to arrive wearing a dessert costume.

Whisk until the mustard pulls the oil and vinegar together. Taste it on a leaf, not straight from the spoon. Dressing that seems aggressively sharp by itself often tastes right once it meets four cups of greens, two pears, and half a cup of cheese.

## What can you swap without wrecking the salad?

Blue cheese is my first choice because its salty bite stands up to the pears. Goat cheese is the easy substitute if blue cheese tastes too forceful. Feta works too, though the salad becomes saltier and less creamy.

Pecans can replace walnuts with no drama. Pistachios work if that's what you have, but chop them roughly so they don't all migrate to the bottom of the bowl. Dried cranberries or pomegranate seeds add tartness, although the salad doesn't need them to function.

## Can you make pear salad ahead of time?

You can do most of the work early. Toast the walnuts, crumble the cheese, and mix the dressing up to two days ahead. Store each part separately in the refrigerator, with the cooled walnuts in a dry container.

Wait to slice the pears and dress the greens until you're ready to eat. If you must cut the fruit early, coat the slices with a little lemon juice and refrigerate them in a sealed container. The color may still change slightly, but they won't look like they spent the afternoon in a waiting room.

Toss gently and serve right away. Pears, walnuts, blue cheese, and balsamic don't need a motivational speech. They just need the dressing added at the last minute.

## How do you turn it into a full lunch?

Add one cup of cooked lentils or chickpeas for four servings, or top each bowl with sliced chicken. The salad already has rich cheese and walnuts, so the extra ingredient should bring substance rather than another layer of fat. A scoop of cooked farro is another good option because its chewy texture can handle the soft pear.

Serve bread on the side if lunch needs more heft. If you want a grain bowl built around similar sweet, sharp, and nutty contrasts, this [farro lunch bowl with lemon tahini](/farro-lunch-bowl-roasted-vegetables-lemon-tahini/) is designed to hold up in a container.

## Keep the portions balanced

Blue cheese and walnuts are assertive ingredients. Half a cup of each across four servings is enough to put some in every bite without burying the pears. Start with three-quarters of the dressing, toss, and add the rest only if the leaves still look dry.

For a larger dinner table, double the greens and pears first, then increase the cheese and walnuts more cautiously. Otherwise the salad gets saltier and heavier instead of simply larger.
