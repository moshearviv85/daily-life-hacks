USDA's latest Thrifty Food Plan says its reference family of four can eat at home for **$235.00 a week**, or **$1,018.20 a month**, based on May 2026 prices. That's $33.57 for the household per day and an average of $8.39 per person per day.

If your last grocery receipt looked long enough to have chapters, $235 raises an obvious question: what went in the cart? USDA built a benchmark, not a shopping list. Useful distinction.

## What is the USDA Thrifty Food Plan?

The [USDA Thrifty Food Plan](https://www.fns.usda.gov/cnpp/thrifty-food-plan-2021) is the government's attempt to answer a very normal question: what would a lower-cost week of food look like if every meal and snack were made at home? USDA maintains four food plans, and Thrifty is the least expensive. Naturally, it's also the one that gets dragged into every argument about whether groceries are "actually" affordable.

The model was rebuilt in 2021 using prices, food data, consumption patterns, and federal dietary guidance. USDA updates the cost monthly with Consumer Price Index categories. That's more honest than recycling an old grocery budget and hoping nobody notices that eggs, coffee, and reality have moved on.

USDA doesn't split the money into four equal envelopes. The report assigns $73.20 to the adult man, $58.20 to the adult woman, $48.00 to the younger child, and $55.50 to the older child. The displayed numbers add to $234.90, while USDA reports $235.00 from unrounded amounts. Ten cents of federal rounding. Nobody is hiding a steak.

## How much does a cheap, healthy week cost in 2026?

For USDA's reference family, the official May 2026 answer is $235.00 a week. Divide it by 7 and you've got $33.57 a day for the household. Spread it across 28 person-days and you've got $8.39 per person per day.

You can push the math one step further. At 3 meals a day, $235 averages about $2.80 per person per meal before snacks. That's an illustration, not a command to make every plate cost exactly $2.80. Breakfast might be oats. Dinner might be chicken. The oatmeal doesn't need to reimburse the chicken.

USDA also adjusts for household size. It suggests adding 20% for 1 person, 10% for 2, and 5% for 3. Four people get no adjustment. Households of 5 or 6 subtract 5%, while 7 or more subtract 10%.

That makes sense once you've cooked for one. A bottle of oil doesn't become one quarter of the price, and cilantro remains a full bunch even when half is planning to liquefy in the crisper drawer.

## What does one week at $235 actually look like?

The 2021 market basket doesn't hand you seven tidy dinners. It hands you categories: 35.37 pounds of vegetables, 26.91 pounds of fruit, 14.13 pounds of grains, 41.30 pounds of dairy foods and alternatives, 16.18 pounds of protein foods, and 7.05 pounds of miscellaneous items per week.

Those are as-purchased weights. Banana peels and the broccoli stalk you ignored count. USDA prices food as it enters the cart, not as it poses on the plate.

At June 2021 prices, protein foods and vegetables each took roughly one quarter of the basket. Grains, dairy, and fruit each took about one seventh. Miscellaneous got the smallest slice, a very official way to acknowledge that condiments exist.

![Bar chart showing the USDA Thrifty Food Plan 2021 market basket cost shares for six food categories](/images/usda-thrifty-food-plan-cost-shares.jpg)

In an actual kitchen, this is a week built from repeatable parts. Oats can become [savory oatmeal bowls with eggs and avocado](/savory-oatmeal-bowls-with-eggs-and-avocado/). Legumes and grains can become [beans and rice](/beans-and-rice-complete-protein-meal/) or a pot of [lentil curry](/lentil-curry-high-fiber-vegan-dinner/). Poultry and grains can meet in an [easy one-pot chicken and rice dinner](/easy-one-pot-chicken-and-rice-dinner/).

The basket gives you ingredients. It doesn't chop the onion or stop somebody from rejecting lentils after you cooked a full pot. That's the gap between a food plan and Tuesday night.

## Where does the model miss real grocery life?

First, the plan isn't a contest to see how long you can live beside a bulk sack of dried beans. USDA includes variety and convenience. Its 2021 basket assumed nearly all beans, peas, and lentils were canned. Our rankings favor dried legumes because they cost less per gram.

USDA is pricing the convenience of opening a can late on Wednesday. We're pricing the food itself. Neither method is wrong. They're measuring two different versions of your evening.

Second, every meal and snack is prepared at home. No takeout. No delivery fee. No coffee grabbed because the morning began with a missing shoe and a dead phone. Our [30-gram fiber day cost study](/what-30-grams-of-fiber-costs-per-day/) shows why that boundary matters: where you buy the meal can move its price far more than the nutrient target does.

Finally, this is a U.S. average for the contiguous states and Washington, DC. Your location, preferences, store access, time, and kitchen equipment can move the real total. A national benchmark is a ruler. It isn't your receipt wearing a government badge.

## How should you use the USDA number?

Use $235 as a comparison point for one very specific family, not as a grade on your shopping. If your household resembles the reference family, compare your food-at-home spending with the weekly figure. If it doesn't, use USDA's age, sex, and household-size method first. Comparing a single adult in Seattle with a four-person national benchmark is spreadsheet theater.

Then look at the gap. Spending more might reflect local prices, convenience, or a teenager approaching the refrigerator like an all-inclusive resort. Spending less might reflect bulk buying or a freezer full of last month's work.

That's what the USDA number is good for. It gives you a credible place to start asking why your total is different. It doesn't get to stand at the checkout and judge the fancy mustard.
