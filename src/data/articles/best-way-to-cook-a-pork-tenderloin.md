ingredients:
  - 1 (1 to 1.5 lb) pork tenderloin
  - 1 tbsp olive oil
  - 1 tsp smoked paprika
  - 1/2 tsp garlic powder
  - 1/2 tsp onion powder
  - 1/2 tsp dried thyme
  - 1/4 tsp cayenne pepper (optional, for a little heat)
  - 1 tsp kosher salt
  - 1/2 tsp black pepper
  - 2 tbsp unsalted butter
  - Fresh parsley, chopped (for garnish)
steps:
  - Preheat your oven to 400°F (200°C).
  - Pat the pork tenderloin completely dry with paper towels. This is crucial for a good sear.
  - In a small bowl, combine the smoked paprika, garlic powder, onion powder, dried thyme, cayenne pepper (if using), salt, and black pepper.
  - Rub the olive oil all over the pork tenderloin, then sprinkle the spice mixture evenly over the entire surface, pressing it gently to adhere.
  - Heat a large, oven-safe skillet (cast iron works great) over medium-high heat until it's very hot, about 2-3 minutes.
  - Add the seasoned pork tenderloin to the hot skillet. Sear undisturbed for 2-3 minutes per side, turning with tongs, until a deep golden-brown crust forms all around.
  - Once seared, add the butter to the skillet. Let it melt and foam, then spoon the melted butter over the tenderloin for about 30 seconds.
  - Carefully transfer the skillet to the preheated oven.
  - Roast for 10-15 minutes, or until an instant-read thermometer inserted into the thickest part of the tenderloin reads 145°F (63°C).
  - Remove the skillet from the oven and transfer the pork tenderloin to a cutting board. Tent loosely with foil and let it rest for at least 5-10 minutes. This resting period is vital for juicy meat.
  - Slice the tenderloin into 1/2-inch thick medallions against the grain.
  - Garnish with fresh chopped parsley and serve immediately.

Pork tenderloin is one of those cuts that promises a lot but often delivers, well, dry disappointment. It's lean, it cooks fast, and it can go from perfectly juicy to sawdust in about 30 seconds if you're not paying attention. But when it's done right? It's tender, flavorful, and feels a lot fancier than the minimal effort it actually takes.

The trick, like with most good things in life, is a combination of high heat and knowing when to quit. You want a good sear for flavor and color, then a quick finish in the oven to cook it through without drying it out. No fancy brine. No complicated technique. Just a thermometer and decent timing. It's the same sear-then-finish logic behind my [pork chops method](/best-way-to-cook-pork-chops/), adapted for a lean little log of meat.

## Why Searing Then Roasting Works

We're going for the best of both worlds here: a screaming hot pan for that beautiful, savory crust, and the even heat of the oven to finish it gently. Searing on the stovetop creates what chefs call the Maillard reaction, basically, all the delicious browning and flavor development that makes meat taste so good. Without that initial sear, your pork would just be kind of pale and sad. Then, moving it to the oven ensures it cooks evenly from all sides without burning the outside, which is a common problem if you try to cook it all the way through on the stovetop.

The other crucial part is the rest. I know, I know, you're hungry. But letting the meat sit for 5-10 minutes after it comes out of the oven allows the juices to redistribute throughout the tenderloin. Cut into it too soon, and all those delicious juices will just run all over your cutting board, leaving you with a dry piece of meat. Don't skip the rest.

## How to Choose and Trim Pork Tenderloin

When you're at the grocery store, look for a tenderloin that's relatively uniform in thickness. Some tenderloins can have a very thin "tail" end. If yours does, you can either tuck it under itself and tie it with kitchen twine to make it thicker, or just lop off the very thin end and save it for another use (like stir-fry). A consistent thickness means it will cook more evenly. Aim for a tenderloin that's between 1 and 1.5 pounds for this recipe; anything larger might need a few more minutes in the oven.

A quick note on silver skin: pork tenderloins usually have a thin, silvery membrane on one side called silver skin. It's tough and doesn't break down when cooked, so it's best to remove it. You can do this by sliding a small, sharp knife under one end of the silver skin, lifting it, and then carefully running the knife between the silver skin and the meat, angling the knife slightly upwards to avoid cutting into the tenderloin itself. Don't stress if you leave a little; it won't ruin your dinner.

## How to Sear Pork Tenderloin Without Steaming It

The key to a good sear is a *hot* pan and dry meat. You want that skillet smoking slightly before you add the oil, and then the oil shimmering before the pork goes in. If your pan isn't hot enough, the pork will just sit there and steam, never developing that deep brown crust. Patting the tenderloin bone-dry with paper towels before you add the rub is also non-negotiable. Any surface moisture will prevent browning. Cast iron is my go-to for this because it holds heat so well, but any heavy-bottomed, oven-safe skillet will do the job. Don't overcrowd the pan if you're cooking multiple tenderloins; sear them in batches if needed.

## Three Pork Tenderloin Seasoning Options

The spice rub in this recipe is a good, all-purpose blend that gives you savory flavor with a hint of warmth from the paprika and cayenne. But pork tenderloin is incredibly versatile. You could go in a totally different direction:
*   **Herby:** Skip the paprika and cayenne, and go heavy on dried rosemary, sage, and a pinch of fennel seeds.
*   **Sweet & Smoky:** Add a tablespoon of brown sugar to the rub for a caramelized crust. Applewood smoked salt would be a nice addition here too.
*   **Spicy Southwest:** More cayenne, a pinch of cumin, and a dash of chili powder would give it a kick.

Don't be afraid to experiment with what you have in your pantry. Just make sure you've got a good amount of salt and pepper in there, as they're essential for flavor.

## Put the Thermometer in the Right Place

Pork tenderloin is narrow, curved, and annoyingly good at giving you a bad reading if you poke it from the top. Slide the thermometer into the thickest section from the side so the tip lands near the center of the meat. Keep it away from the skillet and don't let it pass straight through to the other side. You're measuring pork, not cast iron.

Check more than one spot if the tenderloin has a noticeably thicker end. The thin tail may already be several degrees ahead, which is why folding that tail underneath helps. If you have a leave-in probe, insert it before the skillet goes into the oven. An instant-read thermometer works too; just start checking around the 9-minute mark instead of opening the oven every 90 seconds and wondering why dinner is taking longer.

USDA guidance sets 145°F as the safe minimum for whole cuts of pork, followed by at least a 3-minute rest. This recipe gives it 5-10 minutes. Don't rely on color alone. Modern pork can stay slightly pink at a safe temperature, while overcooked pork can look reassuringly white and chew like a pencil eraser.

## Adjust the Timing for Size and Pan Choice

A 1-pound tenderloin usually finishes faster than a 1.5-pound one, which is obvious until both come in the same package and one looks like it has been training. Start checking the smaller piece first. If you're cooking two tenderloins, leave space between them during the sear. If they touch, the trapped moisture turns the middle of the pan into a steam room.

Cast iron holds heat well, so it recovers quickly after cold meat goes in. Stainless steel also works, but give it a full preheat and don't yank the pork loose before the crust releases naturally. A thin nonstick skillet loses heat faster and may not be rated for a 400°F oven. Check the manufacturer's limit before the handle becomes the most memorable part of dinner.

If your skillet can't go into the oven, move the seared pork to a small baking dish. Warm that dish in the oven first if practical. A refrigerator-cold glass dish isn't the place for hot meat and thermal surprises.

### Make a Quick Pan Sauce While the Pork Rests

The skillet already contains browned bits, butter, and pork drippings. Leaving all that behind would be like paying for checked luggage and flying with an empty suitcase. Put the skillet back over medium heat and add about 1/2 cup of chicken broth, apple cider, or a mixture of the two. Scrape the bottom with a wooden spoon while it bubbles for 2-3 minutes.

For a sharper sauce, stir in 1 teaspoon of Dijon mustard. For something richer, turn off the heat and swirl in another tablespoon of cold butter. Taste before adding salt because the drippings and broth may have handled that job already. You want a loose spoonable sauce, not gravy thick enough to repair drywall.

Slice the pork only after its rest, then spoon the sauce over the medallions. If the board collected a little juice, pour that into the skillet too. It belongs with dinner, not in the sink.

### Plan the Rest of Dinner Around the Hot Oven

The pork spends only 10-15 minutes in the oven, so choose sides that won't demand a separate hour of negotiations. Thin green beans, asparagus, or broccoli can roast at 400°F on a second pan while the pork cooks and rests. Cut dense vegetables such as potatoes or carrots small and start them 20-25 minutes earlier.

Rice, couscous, and a crisp salad also work because they don't compete for oven space. The practical move is to start the starch first, prep the pork while it cooks, and assemble anything cold during the rest. That 5-10 minute pause isn't dead time. It's when you finish the sauce, move vegetables to a bowl, and pretend the kitchen always looked this organized.

## Store and Reheat Pork Tenderloin Without Drying It Out

Refrigerate leftovers within 2 hours in a shallow, covered container. Slice only what you need at dinner if you expect leftovers; a whole piece holds moisture better than a stack of exposed medallions. Use refrigerated cooked pork within 3-4 days.

For reheating, place slices in a covered skillet with a tablespoon or two of broth over low heat. Stop once they're hot. A microwave works, but use 50 percent power in short bursts and cover the pork with a damp paper towel. Full power for three straight minutes is how yesterday's good decisions become today's shoe leather.

Cold slices are useful too. Add them to a sandwich with mustard and pickles, or chop them into fried rice near the end so they warm without cooking all over again. Leftovers don't need a second grand performance. They need a little moisture and less heat than your first instinct suggests.

## Common Pork Tenderloin Mistakes

The first mistake is buying pork loin instead of pork tenderloin. Pork loin is wider, heavier, and needs a different cooking plan. The names are irritatingly similar because grocery labels enjoy small acts of chaos. For this recipe, look for the long, narrow cut that usually weighs 1-1.5 pounds.

The second mistake is leaving the silver skin in place. It won't melt during this short cook, and it can pull the tenderloin into a curve while turning one side chewy. Trim it with a sharp knife, but don't remove every streak of surface fat as if the pork is preparing for a passport photo.

The third mistake is chasing the clock instead of the temperature. Ten minutes in one oven can behave like 14 in another. Tenderloin thickness matters more than the number printed on the package, and carryover heat isn't a license to pull pork far below the safe target. Measure 145°F in the center, rest it, and let the method be boringly reliable.

For a larger dinner, the same thermometer-first thinking matters even more. My [reverse-sear prime rib method](/best-way-to-cook-prime-rib/) takes longer, costs considerably more, and gives you even less reason to guess. For this tenderloin, slice only what you're serving and refrigerate the rest promptly so tomorrow's sandwich still has a chance.
