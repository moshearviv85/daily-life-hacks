ingredients:
- 1 tbsp olive oil
- 1 large yellow onion, chopped
- 2 carrots, chopped
- 2 celery stalks, chopped
- 2 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 cup dried green split peas, rinsed
- 6 cups vegetable broth (or chicken broth)
- 1 tsp dried thyme
- 1/2 tsp smoked paprika (optional)
- Salt and black pepper to taste
- Fresh parsley, chopped (for garnish)
steps:
- Heat olive oil in a large pot or Dutch oven over medium heat. Add onion, carrots, and celery. Sauté for 5 to 7 minutes until softened.
- Stir in minced garlic and cook for another minute until fragrant.
- Add the rinsed split peas, vegetable broth, dried thyme, and smoked paprika (if using) to the pot.
- Bring the soup to a boil, then reduce heat to low, cover, and simmer for 35 to 45 minutes, or until peas are tender and the soup has thickened. Stir occasionally so nothing sticks.
- Season with salt and black pepper.
- Ladle into bowls, garnish with fresh parsley, and serve hot.

Sometimes you just need a big bowl of something warm. Not a trendy smoothie. Not a salad that looks like it was designed for a photoshoot. Real food that sits in your stomach like it means it.

When the wind starts whistling and daylight clocks out at 3:30 PM, my brain flips to soup mode. And not just any soup. The classics. The kind that smells like somebody's grandma was in charge even if your grandma never made this exact pot. Split pea.

Yeah, I know. Peas. They sound plain. Then they cook down into a thick, savory situation that feels like a blanket with a spoon. One hearty bowl of this high fiber split pea soup can land around 16 grams of fiber. That's not a cute garnish number. That's real grocery math. And dried split peas are cheap enough that the whole pot barely registers on the bill.

## Why is split pea soup so satisfying?

There's something deeply satisfying about turning dried peas, a few humble vegetables, and broth into a meal that tastes like it simmered all afternoon. You chop. You toss. You wait. That's most of the skill.

## The fiber numbers without the lecture

People ask how much fiber in a cup of split pea soup because the label math is weird when you're cooking from dried peas. Here's the practical version.

Dried split peas are fiber dense before they ever hit the pot. As they soften, they thicken the broth, so a ladle of finished soup carries more plant material than a thin vegetable broth. A cup often lands in the 8 to 10 gram range. A full bowl closer to two cups can push toward 16 grams or more if you kept the soup thick and included the carrots and celery.

We ranked common foods by cost in the [fiber per dollar study](/fiber-per-dollar-cheapest-high-fiber-foods/). Dried legumes keep embarrassing almost everything with a pretty package. Split peas belong in that same boring-hero category. You're not buying fiber theater. You're buying a bag of peas.

Increase fiber gradually if your baseline diet is mostly white bread and takeout. Jumping from almost none to a giant bowl every night is how people decide legumes are the enemy. One bowl, water nearby, done.

## Building flavor without a ham bone

Classic diner versions lean on smoked meat. Fine if you have leftovers. Unnecessary if you don't.

**Onion, carrot, celery** are the base. Soften them in oil until the onion turns translucent and the kitchen smells like you meant to cook. That step is doing more work than people give it credit for.

**Garlic** goes in late so it doesn't burn and turn bitter.

**Thyme** gives the soup that "I've been on the stove awhile" vibe even when you started at 5:40 PM.

**Smoked paprika** is the fake-ham trick. A half teaspoon is enough. You're not making barbecue.

Broth quality matters more than people want to admit. A flat broth makes flat soup. Use something you'd actually sip. Chicken broth if you eat it, vegetable if you don't. Salt at the end after the peas soften, because the liquid reduces and concentrates.

## Texture: creamy without a blender drama

Split peas break down on their own. That's the whole point of buying them split. Simmer until they're soft and the soup looks thick enough that a spoon leaves a brief trail.

Want it smoother? Mash a few scoops against the side of the pot, or blitz a cup with an immersion blender and stir it back in. Want it chunkier? Stop sooner and leave more intact peas. There is no single correct texture, only the one you'll eat again tomorrow.

If it gets too thick in the fridge overnight, that's normal. Add a splash of water or broth when you reheat. Day-old split pea soup is basically the same meal wearing a denser sweater.

## What to serve with it (and what to skip)

**Good partners:**

- Crusty bread for dragging through the bowl
- A simple green salad if you want crunch next to all that softness
- Leftover rice stirred in if you need more calories and bulk for a hungry table

**Skip the guilt sides:** you don't need a second protein project on top unless you want it. Split peas already bring plant protein along with the fiber. A grilled cheese on the side is allowed. This is dinner, not a purity contest.

If you're feeding people who claim they "don't like peas," don't announce the ingredient list like a TED Talk. Call it green soup, put bread on the table, and let the bowl do the talking.

## Batch cooking and storage rules

This recipe scales cleanly. Double the peas and broth, use a bigger pot, same method. Cool it before you lid it for the fridge. Warm soup in a sealed container is how you get condensation and weird texture.

Fridge: about four days.

Freezer: up to three months in portions. Leave headspace because it expands. Thaw overnight in the fridge, then reheat with liquid to loosen.

I freeze single lunch portions in jars or containers so future-me has a microwaveable bowl that didn't come from a drive-through. Future-me is usually grateful and slightly smug.

## Small tweaks that actually change the pot

- **Extra vegetables:** diced potato, a handful of spinach at the end, or leftover roasted carrots stirred in.
- **Heat:** red pepper flakes if you want a little bite.
- **Acidity:** a squeeze of lemon or a tiny splash of vinegar at the end brightens a heavy bowl. Sounds odd. Works.
- **Meat:** diced leftover ham or a smoked sausage coin if that's your household's love language. Brown it first for better flavor.
- **Creaminess:** a spoon of olive oil at the table, not a cup of cream in the pot. Keep it simple.

Don't overcomplicate a dish whose whole personality is "peas got soft and delicious." This recipe is not trying to win a chef competition. It's trying to get fiber on the table before anyone asks for cereal.

## Common mistakes that flatten the soup

**Not rinsing the peas.** A quick rinse knocks off dust. Takes ten seconds.

**Boiling hard the whole time.** A hard boil can scorch the bottom and make the peas cook unevenly. Simmer. Stir. Be patient.

**Salting too early with a salty broth.** Taste at the end. You can always add. You can't un-salt a reduced pot.

**Expecting thin soup to feel filling.** If it's watery, keep simmering uncovered for a bit, or mash some peas. Thickness is part of why this bowl satisfies.

**Serving it with zero liquid elsewhere in the day.** Fiber works better when you're also drinking water or tea. Soup helps, but don't treat the bowl as your only fluid plan.
