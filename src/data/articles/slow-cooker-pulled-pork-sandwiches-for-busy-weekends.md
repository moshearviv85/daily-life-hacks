ingredients:
  - 3-4 lb boneless pork shoulder (Boston butt)
  - 1 tbsp smoked paprika
  - 1 tbsp garlic powder
  - 1 tsp onion powder
  - 1 tsp black pepper
  - 1/2 tsp cayenne pepper (optional, for a little kick)
  - 1 tsp salt
  - 1 cup chicken broth
  - 1/2 cup apple cider vinegar
  - 1/4 cup Worcestershire sauce
  - 1/4 cup brown sugar, packed
  - 1 cup barbecue sauce, plus more for serving
  - 8-10 hamburger buns or rolls
  - Coleslaw, for serving (optional)
  - Pickles, for serving (optional)
steps:
  - Trim any excess large pieces of fat from the pork shoulder.
  - In a small bowl, mix together the smoked paprika, garlic powder, onion powder, black pepper, cayenne pepper (if using), and salt.
  - Rub the spice mixture all over the pork shoulder, ensuring it's well coated.
  - Place the seasoned pork shoulder in the slow cooker.
  - In a separate bowl, whisk together the chicken broth, apple cider vinegar, Worcestershire sauce, and brown sugar. Pour this liquid mixture over the pork in the slow cooker.
  - Cover and cook on low for 6-8 hours, or on high for 3-4 hours, until the pork is fork-tender and shreds easily.
  - Once cooked, carefully remove the pork from the slow cooker and place it on a large cutting board or in a shallow dish. Reserve about 1 cup of the cooking liquid from the slow cooker.
  - Using two forks, shred the pork into small pieces. Discard any remaining large pieces of fat.
  - Return the shredded pork to the slow cooker. Add the 1 cup of reserved cooking liquid and the barbecue sauce. Stir to combine thoroughly.
  - Cook on low for another 30 minutes to allow the flavors to meld and the sauce to warm through.
  - Serve the warm pulled pork on hamburger buns, topped with coleslaw and pickles if desired.

  - question: "Can I use a different cut of pork for this recipe?"
    answer: While pork shoulder (Boston butt) is ideal for its fat content and tenderness, you could use pork loin or tenderloin. Just be aware they are leaner and might dry out if overcooked, so adjust cooking time.
  - question: "How long does slow cooker pulled pork last in the fridge?"
    answer: Leftover pulled pork, stored in an airtight container, will keep well in the refrigerator for up to 3-4 days. It reheats nicely in the microwave or on the stovetop.
  - question: "Can I freeze pulled pork?"
    answer: Yes, absolutely. Once cooled, place the pulled pork in freezer-safe bags or containers. It can be frozen for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge before reheating.
  - question: "What are some other ways to serve pulled pork besides sandwiches?"

  - question: "My pulled pork isn't shredding easily. What should I do?"
    answer: This usually means it needs more cooking time. Return it to the slow cooker and continue cooking on low until it's falling apart with just a gentle tug of a fork. Patience is key for tender results.

Weekends are for doing anything but standing over a hot stove for hours. That's a hill I will die on. If you're like me, you want to get out, run errands, maybe even relax a little, without sacrificing a good, hearty meal. This is where the slow cooker becomes your best friend. Specifically, when it's loaded with a big, glorious hunk of pork shoulder.

This isn't just about convenience; it's about flavor. The slow cooker method practically guarantees a fork-tender, juicy pulled pork that's drenched in a savory, slightly sweet, and tangy sauce. It's the kind of meal that makes your house smell amazing all day, and then delivers on that promise come dinner time. You do a little prep in the morning, forget about it, and come back to a meal that tastes like you put in way more effort than you actually did.

### Why Pork Shoulder is the Only Cut for Pulled Pork

Look, I've tried other cuts in a pinch, but nothing beats a good boneless pork shoulder, also known as Boston butt. It's got enough fat marbled through it to stay incredibly moist during those long cooking times. That fat slowly renders, basting the meat from the inside out, and giving you that rich, melt-in-your-mouth texture that is the hallmark of truly good pulled pork. Leaner cuts like pork loin just don't have the same staying power; they tend to dry out before they get that fall-apart tender. Don't compromise on the cut here. It matters.

### Getting the Flavor Right: The Rub and the Liquid

The magic of this recipe starts with the spice rub. We're talking smoked paprika for depth, garlic and onion powder for that foundational savory base, and a touch of cayenne if you like a little heat. Don't skip the rub; it creates a crust of flavor that permeates the whole piece of meat.

Then there's the liquid. You need a mix that provides moisture and tang. Chicken broth adds a savory base, apple cider vinegar cuts through the richness of the pork, and Worcestershire sauce brings a complex umami note. A bit of brown sugar balances it all out. This isn't just about keeping the pork from drying out; it's about building layers of flavor that cook down into a delicious, concentrated sauce.

### Timing is Everything (But Not in a Stressful Way)

The beauty of the slow cooker is its flexibility. Six to eight hours on low, or three to four on high. What you're looking for is "fork-tender." This isn't a suggestion; it's a requirement. The pork should practically fall apart when you poke it with a fork. If it resists, it's not ready. Just put the lid back on and give it more time. There's very little risk of overcooking it to the point of dryness when it's braising in all that liquid. When it's done, you'll pull it out, shred it, and then return it to the slow cooker with some of that reserved cooking liquid and your favorite barbecue sauce. This last step is crucial for getting all those flavors to really soak into every strand of pork.

### Serving Suggestions Beyond the Bun

While a classic pulled pork sandwich on a soft bun with coleslaw and pickles is a masterpiece, don't limit yourself. This recipe makes a good amount, so you'll likely have leftovers. Think pulled pork tacos with a fresh salsa, piled high on a baked potato, or even as a topping for homemade nachos. It's also excellent mixed into mac and cheese for a truly indulgent meal. The point is, this isn't just a one-hit wonder.

This slow cooker pulled pork is a real workhorse for busy households. It demands minimal attention but delivers maximum flavor and comfort. It's the kind of meal that makes you feel like you've really accomplished something delicious, even when your weekend schedule is packed.
