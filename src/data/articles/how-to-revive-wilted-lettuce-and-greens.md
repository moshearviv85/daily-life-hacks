Soak wilted lettuce in ice water for **5 to 10 minutes**, then dry it thoroughly. That's the whole rescue operation. No lemon juice, no special powder, and no solemn goodbye beside the trash can.

This works when the lettuce is limp because it has lost water. It doesn't work when the lettuce is slimy, smelly, or turning black. At that point, the ice isn't performing a comeback. It's just making cold garbage.

| What the lettuce looks like | What to do | Time |
|---|---|---:|
| Limp but clean and fresh-smelling | Soak in ice water, then dry | 5 to 10 minutes |
| Sturdy romaine that's still floppy | Trim the base, soak, then dry | Up to 15 minutes |
| Slimy, mushy, sour-smelling, or blackened | Discard it | 0 minutes |

*Source: [UC San Diego Center for Community Health](https://communityhealth.ucsd.edu/work/eat-ca/salad-greens) recommends a 5-to-10-minute ice-water soak for wilted greens. [Illinois Extension](https://extension.illinois.edu/gardening/preparing-lettuce) also recommends ice water for limp lettuce.*

## How do you revive wilted lettuce step by step?

You need a large bowl, cold water, ice, and something that can actually dry the leaves afterward. The salad spinner is doing more useful work here than any $40 countertop gadget with a motivational name.

1. **Sort the leaves.** Pull out anything slimy, mushy, badly discolored, or sour-smelling. Don't negotiate with rot.
2. **Separate and trim.** Pull apart lettuce leaves so the cold water can reach them. For a whole head of romaine, trim a thin slice from the dry base.
3. **Fill a bowl with cold water and ice.** You want genuinely cold water, not three lonely cubes floating in room-temperature optimism.
4. **Soak for 5 to 10 minutes.** Check delicate greens at 5 minutes. Give sturdy romaine a little longer if it still bends like wet paper.
5. **Drain and dry completely.** Use a salad spinner or clean kitchen towels. Dressing sticks to dry leaves. Wet leaves turn your vinaigrette into expensive puddle water.
6. **Use the lettuce soon.** The ice bath buys you another meal. It doesn't restart the lettuce's warranty.

Once it's crisp, put it somewhere it matters. A [pear salad with walnuts](/pear-salad-with-walnuts-fiber-rich/) is a better ending than letting the same bag wilt again on Friday.

## Why does ice water make limp lettuce crisp again?

Lettuce is mostly water, and it loses crispness as moisture leaves the tissue. UC Davis notes that lettuce is prone to water loss after harvest and that the outer leaves soften as that happens. A short soak lets limp leaves take up water again, while the cold temperature makes the whole process useful for salad instead of producing warm, soggy greens.

That's also why freezing isn't the clever shortcut it sounds like. Freezing damages the texture of high-water vegetables. Thawed lettuce is excellent if your goal is to make lettuce feel personally betrayed, but it's not coming back as a crisp salad.

## Which greens can you rescue with an ice bath?

The method works best on greens that are limp but otherwise sound:

- Romaine and iceberg usually show the most obvious improvement.
- Green leaf and red leaf lettuce can perk up quickly.
- Kale and chard may need closer to 10 or 15 minutes.
- Spinach and arugula are delicate, so check them at 5 minutes.
- Parsley and cilantro can recover well after you trim the stem ends.

Spring mix is the unreliable roommate of the group. Some leaves may recover while others have already crossed into mush. Sort it before soaking, then dry it gently.

## When should you throw wilted lettuce away?

Wilting and spoilage aren't the same thing. A limp leaf that's still clean, intact, and fresh-smelling may be worth rescuing. A slimy leaf isn't thirsty. It's finished.

Throw the lettuce away if you find:

- A sour, rotten, or fermented smell
- Slime across the leaves or inside the bag
- Mushy tissue that tears when you touch it
- Widespread black or brown decay
- Visible mold

Don't use the best-looking half of a bag if liquid from rotten leaves has coated everything else. Saving $2.49 isn't a personality test.

## How do you keep lettuce from wilting again?

The second rescue is prevention. Dry the leaves well, store them in a container or bag with a paper towel to catch extra moisture, and keep them in the refrigerator's high-humidity drawer if yours has one. Too little moisture makes lettuce limp. Too much loose water encourages the exact slimy situation we're trying to avoid.

If you're packing lunch, keep dressing away from the leaves until you're ready to eat.
