I used to stand in front of bakery cases feeling personally attacked by seven-dollar loaves. Beautiful bread. Ridiculous price. Meanwhile the grocery sandwich bread tasted like sweetened foam and went weirdly sticky by day four. I didn't want a sourdough pet named Gladys. I wanted toast that didn't taste like packaging, and a PB&J that didn't fall apart mid-bite.

Bread baking gets sold like a personality upgrade. Proofing baskets. Steam pans. Lectures about grams versus cups. Fine for people who bake for sport. This easy sandwich bread recipe for beginners is for everyone else: one loaf pan, a bag of flour, and a couple of hours on a Sunday while laundry runs in the background.

## Why this loaf works for normal kitchens

A lot of beginner bread fails because the recipe assumes you already know what "smooth and elastic" feels like, or because it's fussy about equipment you don't own. This dough is forgiving. Measuring cups are allowed. Hands are allowed. A stand mixer is optional, not a membership card.

The melted butter is doing real work. Fat softens the crumb so the crust doesn't shatter like a cracker when you spread peanut butter. You want a loaf that bends a little, toasts evenly, and survives a packed lunch without turning into crumbs at the bottom of a bag.

If you're building a wider Sunday routine around food that lasts the week, pair baking day with a simple [meal prep system for beginners](/meal-prep-for-beginners-complete-system/). Bread cools while chicken and rice finish. Future-you gets sandwiches instead of another sad desk salad negotiation.

## Ingredients, without the gatekeeping

- 3 cups all-purpose flour, plus a little for dusting
- 1 cup warm water, about 110°F
- 2 1/4 teaspoons active dry yeast (one standard packet)
- 1 tablespoon sugar
- 1 1/2 teaspoons salt
- 2 tablespoons unsalted butter, melted
- 1 tablespoon oil for the bowl

That's it. No milk powder. No dough conditioner. No specialty flour you have to hunt down.

Water temperature matters more than people admit. Too hot and you murder the yeast. Too cold and the rise drags forever. Aim for bathwater warm, not tea-scalding. If you don't have a thermometer, stick a clean finger in. It should feel warm, not "why did I do that."

Sugar feeds the yeast and helps browning. Salt keeps the flavor from tasting like warm nothing. Butter keeps the slice soft. Skip the butter and you'll get a leaner, drier loaf that still works for toast but fights you on sandwiches.

## Step-by-step: mix, knead, rise, bake

**1. Wake up the yeast.** Whisk warm water, sugar, and yeast in a large bowl. Wait 5 to 10 minutes. You want foam on top, like a weak latte. No foam? Yeast is dead or water was wrong. New packet. New start. Don't pour flour into a lifeless bowl and hope.

**2. Make a shaggy dough.** Stir in melted butter and salt. Add flour one cup at a time. Mix with a wooden spoon until it clumps into a rough dough. It should look ugly. Ugly is correct at this stage.

**3. Knead until it behaves.** Turn onto a lightly floured counter. Knead 8 to 10 minutes. Push, fold, turn, repeat. Early on it sticks and tears. Keep going. It should smooth out and spring back when you poke it. If it's glue, add flour a teaspoon at a time. If it's a dry brick, wet your hands and keep kneading.

Stand mixer option: dough hook on low-medium for about 6 to 8 minutes. Same end goal. Smooth, elastic, slightly tacky.

**4. First rise.** Oil a bowl. Plop the dough in. Cover with a damp cloth or plastic wrap. Park it somewhere warm for 1 to 1.5 hours until doubled. Cold kitchen? Use the oven with just the light on. Don't turn the heat on unless you enjoy yeast tragedy.

**5. Shape the loaf.** Punch down to release the big gas bubbles. Pat into a rough rectangle, then roll into a log about the length of your 9x5-inch pan. Pinch the seam. Place seam-side down in a greased pan.

**6. Second rise.** Cover and let it rise about 45 minutes until the dough crowns just above the pan rim. Preheat the oven to 375°F while you wait. Baking into a cold oven is how you get pale, dense disappointment.

**7. Bake.** 30 to 35 minutes. Top should be deep golden. Tap the bottom of the loaf after you tip it out. Hollow sound usually means done. Internal temperature around 190 to 200°F is a useful backup if you have an instant-read thermometer.

**8. Cool completely.** This is the hard part. Pull it from the pan right away so the sides don't steam soggy. Cool on a rack at least an hour before slicing. Cut too early and the crumb gums up like you made bread pudding by accident.

## What "kneaded enough" actually feels like

Beginners stop too soon because their arms are tired, or they keep going until the dough is a dry flour rock. Target the middle.

Under-kneaded dough tears when you stretch a small piece. The baked loaf can look dense and crumbly. Over-floured dough feels stiff and bakes dry. Proper dough stretches without ripping immediately and feels smooth, not sticky soup and not Play-Doh left in the sun.

If mid-knead you're losing the will to live, cover the dough for ten minutes. That rest lets gluten relax. Come back and finish. Bread doesn't require continuous suffering.

## Rising problems and boring fixes

No rise: dead yeast, icy kitchen, or salt dumped straight onto yeast before it dissolved. Proof the yeast first next time. Give cold dough more time instead of assuming failure at the one-hour mark.

Rose then collapsed: overproofed. Next bake, catch it earlier. You can still bake a slightly overproofed loaf. Expect a flatter top.

Dense middle with a pretty crust: underbaked or shaped with big air pockets you never pressed out. Bake a few minutes longer, and roll the log tighter next round.

Pale loaf: oven not fully preheated, or rack too low. Middle rack, fully hot oven, golden means golden.

## Slicing, toasting, and sandwich reality

Use a serrated knife. Saw gently. Don't crush the loaf like you're mad at carbs. Medium slices for sandwiches. Thinner for toast if your toaster has opinions.

This crumb holds turkey, cheese, and mustard without dissolving. It also makes excellent grilled cheese because the buttered exterior crisps while the middle stays soft. French toast works if you've got a day-old end piece.

For lunches, toast or not is personal. Untoasted stays softer in a bag. Toasted travels better if you cool it fully before wrapping so steam doesn't turn it rubbery.

## How to store it so you're not eating stale sadness

Homemade sandwich bread stales faster than the grocery foam brick. That's normal. No mystery preservatives.

Day one and two: airtight bag or bread box at room temperature. Keep it away from the sunny window and the stove.

Past day three: freeze. Slice first. Pull what you need. Toast from frozen. For the full storage playbook, use [how to store homemade bread](/how-to-store-homemade-bread/). If mold is your recurring villain, [keeping bread fresh longer without mold](/how-to-keep-bread-fresh-longer-without-mold/) covers the fridge myth and the freezer method in more detail.

Never refrigerate the loaf "to keep it fresh." The fridge speeds staling. Soft bread turns dry and sad in a hurry. Counter for a couple days. Freezer after that.

## Small swaps that still count as this recipe

Milk instead of water makes a slightly richer crumb. Use the same cup measure, still warm.

Honey instead of sugar works. Flavor shifts a little sweeter.

Olive oil instead of butter is fine in a pinch. Texture stays soft enough for sandwiches.

Half whole wheat, half all-purpose: add a splash more water if the dough feels stiff. Pure 100% whole wheat is a different project with a denser personality. Start with this white loaf first so you know what success feels like.

## Make it part of the week, not a performance

Bake on Sunday. Cool while you clean the counter. Slice once it's fully cool. Bag half for the counter. Freeze half for Wednesday toast. That's the whole system.

You don't need a bakery identity. You need a loaf that costs less than the fancy counter bread, tastes like actual bread, and shows up for sandwiches without drama. One bowl. One pan. One packet of yeast. That's enough.
