ingredients:
  - "3 cups all-purpose flour (plus more for dusting)"
  - "1 cup warm water (about 110°F)"
  - "2 1/4 tsp active dry yeast (one packet)"
  - "1 tbsp granulated sugar"
  - "1 1/2 tsp salt"
  - "2 tbsp unsalted butter, melted"
  - "1 tbsp vegetable oil (for the bowl)"
steps:
  - "In a large bowl, whisk the warm water, sugar, and yeast together. Let it sit for 5 to 10 minutes until it looks foamy."
  - "Stir in the melted butter and salt. Gradually add the flour, one cup at a time, mixing with a wooden spoon until a shaggy dough forms."
  - "Turn the dough onto a floured surface and knead for 8 to 10 minutes until smooth and elastic. If it sticks to your hands, add a teaspoon of flour at a time."
  - "Place dough in a lightly oiled bowl, cover with a damp cloth, and let rise in a warm spot for about 1 to 1.5 hours or until doubled in size."
  - "Punch the dough down to release air. Shape it into a log and place it into a greased 9x5 inch loaf pan."
  - "Cover and let rise again for 45 minutes. Preheat your oven to 375°F during this time."
  - "Bake for 30 to 35 minutes until the top is golden brown and the loaf sounds hollow when tapped on the bottom."
  - "Remove from the pan immediately and let cool completely on a wire rack before slicing."

I used to be the person who stared at the artisan sourdough loaves in the bakery window with a mix of awe and genuine resentment. Who has four days to "feed" a starter named Gladys? Not me, and probably not you. I just wanted a piece of toast that didn't taste like cardboard or cost seven dollars a loaf.

The truth is that bread baking has been over-complicated by people who want to sell you expensive proofing baskets. You don't need a degree in chemistry or a stone oven. This easy sandwich bread recipe for beginners is designed for the person who has a standard loaf pan, a bag of flour, and a couple of hours on a Sunday afternoon while the laundry is running.

## Why this recipe works for normal people

Most bread recipes fail because they're too fussy about weight. While professional bakers swear by grams, most of us just want to use a measuring cup and get on with our lives. This dough is forgiving. It's meant to be handled by human hands, not just a stand mixer.

The secret to a soft crust is the fat content. By adding a little melted butter, we ensure the bread stays pliable enough for a peanut butter and jelly sandwich without shattering like a cracker. It's a basic white loaf that actually holds up to a toaster.
