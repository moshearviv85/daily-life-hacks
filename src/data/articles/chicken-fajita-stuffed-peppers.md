steps:
  - Preheat oven to 375°F (190°C). Cut the tops off the 4 bell peppers and remove seeds and membranes. Trim the tops to make them neat, then dice the tops (you'll use them in the filling). Place peppers in a baking dish cut-side up.
  - Season the chicken strips with fajita seasoning, salt, and pepper. Heat 1 tablespoon olive oil in a large skillet over medium-high heat. Cook chicken in batches until just cooked through, about 3-4 minutes per side. Remove and set aside.
  - Add the remaining olive oil to the same skillet. Sauté the onion and sliced bell peppers (from the tops and extra) until softened and slightly caramelized, about 5-6 minutes. Add more fajita seasoning if needed.

Here's the thing about stuffed peppers: they look impressive enough for company but simple enough for a Tuesday dinner, and that gap is where they live rent-free in my kitchen rotation. These chicken fajita stuffed peppers specifically solve a problem I run into constantly which is wanting fajitas but not wanting to deal with folding a tortilla while it falls apart or standing at a hot grill outside in February. You get the flavors, you get thetexure, and you get something that holds together on a plate without requiring a napkin on your lap.

The other thing worth saying upfront is that this is a forgiving recipe. If you overcook the chicken slightly in the skillet, it's fine because it's going back in the oven anyway. If your fajita seasoning is a year old, add a little more and taste as you go. The peppers themselves carry so much of the dish that small variations in the filling won't sink the whole thing.

## Picking Your Peppers

The pepper choice matters more than people admit. You want large, thick-walled bell peppers that can hold a decent amount of filling without collapsing. The standard supermarket bell pepper works fine, but if you can find the ones that are still attached to the vine, grab them. They taste better and hold their shape better, probably because they're fresher.

Any color works, and honestly mixing colors looks better on the plate. The green ones are slightly more bitter and hold their shape a little better in the oven, while red and yellow get sweeter as they cook. A mix of two reds, one yellow, and one green gives you visual interest and slight textural variation.

When you're cutting the tops off, don't just saw through straight across. Angle your knife slightly so you're creating a wider opening at the top while keeping the bottom relatively narrow. This helps them stand up better and lets you pack more filling in without it overflowing. Save those tops, by the way. Dice them up and throw them into the filling. It adds volume and flavor and means you're not wasting anything.

The seeds are your enemy here. Get in there with your fingers or a small spoon and remove every last bit of the white membrane too, since that's where the bitter taste lives. A quick rinse after seeding helps get rid of any remaining seeds.

## The Chicken and Fajita Situation

For the chicken, thin strips are non-negotiable. If you buy thick chicken breasts, slice them in half horizontally before cutting into strips. You're looking for pieces about a half-inch wide and three inches long. The thinness matters because you're going to cook them in a skillet first, and thick pieces will overcook on the outside before cooking through.

A well-seasoned cast iron skillet gives you the best browning, but any large skillet works. Don't crowd the pan. If your skillet isn't big enough, cook the chicken in two batches. The crowding is what steams the chicken instead of searing it, and you want that sear for flavor. The fond that builds up in the bottom of the pan after you remove the chicken gets scraped up when you cook the onions and peppers next, and that is where a lot of the fajita flavor lives.

Speaking of fajita seasoning, I don't shame anyone for the store-bought stuff. Old El Paso, McCormick, whatever's on sale. They're consistent and fine. If you want to make your own, a basic blend of chili powder, cumin, garlic powder, paprika, onion powder, salt, and a little cayenne will get you there. Use about two tablespoons of whatever seasoning you're using for this amount of chicken, maybe a little more if you like it bold.

The key step a lot of people skip is properly seasoning the chicken and letting it sit for a few minutes after seasoning. Ten minutes is ideal, but even five helps the seasoning adhere and the chicken come closer to room temperature before it hits the hot pan.

## Building the Filling

Once your chicken is cooked and set aside, you want the skillet nice and hot still. Add a little more oil if needed, then throw in your onions and those extra bell peppers you sliced. You're not trying to fully cook them here. You want them softened and slightly caramelized with some color, which takes about five to six minutes over medium-high heat. Stir occasionally but not constantly. Letting them sit gives them a chance to brown.

While that's happening, get your rice ready. Day-old rice is actually better for this because it's dried out a little and won't make the filling soggy. If you're cooking rice fresh, use slightly less water than normal so it comes out a little firmer. About one cup of cooked rice is right for four peppers.

The beans are straightforward. Drain and rinse black beans. You could use pinto beans if you prefer, but black beans hold their shape better in the oven and have a more distinct flavor that stands up to the fajita seasoning. Don't mash them. Whole beans are better.

Now here's where you combine everything. Dice the chicken into bite-sized pieces first, then mix it with the sautéed vegetables, rice, beans, half the cheese, and the salsa. The salsa adds moisture and extra flavor, and using a chunky salsa gives you little bits of tomato and pepper throughout the filling. Give it a taste. Does it need more salt? More fajita seasoning? Adjust now because the filling is going into the peppers and you can't fix it after.

## The Cooking Method

Stuffing the peppers is a satisfying process if you do it right. Pack the filling in firmly with a spoon, pressing down as you go. You want it dense in there, not loosely piled. The filling will settle as it cooks, and you want them full at the start so they don't look deflated when they come out of the oven. Overfill slightly if needed. It's better to have a little overflow than to have half-empty peppers.

The baking dish matters. A nine-by-thirteen works for four peppers side by side. You want them snug but not so tight that they're touching each other completely, since you want some air circulation around the sides. If they're wobbling, use that foil ball trick I mentioned earlier to prop them up.

Covering with foil for the first twenty-five minutes is essential. The peppers need to steam and soften before the cheese goes on top. Without the cover, the tops would burn before the bottoms cooked through. After twenty-five minutes, remove the foil, add the remaining cheese to each pepper, and put them back in for ten more minutes. The cheese should be melted, bubbly, and starting to brown in spots.

Total cook time at three hundred seventy-five degrees is about thirty-five to forty minutes. If your peppers are on the smaller side, check at thirty. If they're huge, they might need forty-five. The fork test is your friend here. Pierce the side of a pepper near the bottom. If it goes in easily and the pepper feels tender, you're done.

## Substitutions That Actually Work

This recipe is flexible enough that you can bend it without breaking it. The chicken can swap for ground beef cooked with the same seasoning, or shredded rotisserie chicken if you want to skip the stovetop entirely. Using rotisserie chicken means you can make this even on a night when you don't want to stand over a hot pan.

For the rice, cauliflower rice works if you're avoiding grains. You'll need to squeeze out excess moisture before adding it to the mix, and maybe add a little extra salsa for moisture since cauliflower rice doesn't hold liquid the same way. The texture is different but it works.

No black beans? Pinto beans are fine. No beans at all? Add an extra half cup of rice or throw in some frozen corn. The corn adds sweetness that plays well against the fajita spices.

Dairy-free? Skip the cheese or use a dairy-free shred. The dish is still plenty flavorful without it, though you lose that bubbly top that makes it look so appealing. You could put a dairy-free cheese on top for the last ten minutes, but honestly, skipping the cheese entirely and adding a squeeze of lime and some fresh cilantro at the end works just as well.

## Storing and Reheating

These keep well, which makes them a solid meal prep option. After they cool completely, wrap each pepper individually in plastic wrap, then put them in a zip-top bag. They'll last in the refrigerator for four days. In the freezer, you can get away with two months, though the peppers will be softer when thawed. Freezer burn isn't a huge risk if they're wrapped tightly.

Reheating from refrigerated is easy. Microwave works when you're in a hurry. Put a damp paper towel over the peppers so they don't dry out, heat for two to three minutes, then check if they're heated through. If not, another minute. The oven is better if you have time. Three hundred fifty degrees, covered with foil, for fifteen to twenty minutes. Uncover for the last five if you want the cheese to get crispy again.

From frozen, thaw overnight in the refrigerator, then reheat using the oven method. Microwaving from frozen is possible but uneven. The edges get hot while the center stays cold, and you run the risk of the pepper skin getting rubbery.

## Serving Suggestions

A few garnishes take these from good to great. A drizzle of sour cream right on top of the melted cheese adds creaminess that cuts through the fajita spices. Fresh cilantro, if you like it, adds brightness. A squeeze of lime over everything right before eating wakes up all the flavors.

These are substantial enough to stand alone as a meal, but if you want sides, Spanish rice and refried beans are the obvious choices. A simple green salad with a lime vinaigrette would balance the richness. The plate should feel complete without requiring a whole second dish.

One thing I do sometimes is serve the peppers with a few warm tortillas on the side. You're not eating the fajitas in tortilla form, but having a small tortilla to scoop up any filling that falls out is a nice option, especially if you're feeding kids who might be skeptical of the peppers themselves.

If you're making these for a group where people have different preferences, set up a toppings bar. Sour cream, guacamole, extra salsa, jalapeños, hot sauce, cilantro, lime wedges. Everyone can customize their pepper the way they like it, and it makes dinner feel a little more like an event.

Now, the real test is whether these hold up to being a legitimate dinner option and not just a fun novelty. And honestly, they do. The combination of the sweet tender pepper, the seasoned chicken and rice filling, the beans for texture, and the melted cheese on top works as a complete dish. It's faster than it looks, it uses one main pan plus a baking dish, and it feeds four people without any side dish stress. That's the kind of weeknight dinner worth having in rotation.
